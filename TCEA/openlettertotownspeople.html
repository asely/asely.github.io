Traditional Church of England in Ashford

“That even now, while we are placed among things that are passing away; to cleave to those which shall abide”

A very important note from the founder and co-ordinator

To all dear friends in the preservation of our heritage,

For all that you have accomplished so far in the preservation of tradition, I must thank you most heartily. We now have a battle so important that, should we lose, our group will have entirely lost the fight to preserve tradition in the Parish Church of St Mary-the –Virgin, Ashford, and the rich tapestry of heritage as we know it, and as built up in this town and nurtured since the days of Canon Alcock (1847) will pass utterly into history.

THE PEWS.

Many of you will know of the plans to remove all the pews from the Nave of the Church. Those pews have been part of the church since 1879, when they replaced a set of Georgian pews (these Georgian Pews remain in the galleries on the South, West and North walls.) The pews in the nave give the church a very special atmosphere. It is hard to believe that anyone should want to remove them, but this, I am afraid, is exactly what the leadership want to do.

The precise situation is as follows:

+ In 1999 the four pews (two on each side) and front rails were removed from the nave. I have consulted the Dicocesan Adviser regarding this. No Faculty license was obtained for this, but an Archdeacons Licence was granted. This is not a permanent license (though there is no time limit on it) but it was envisaged that the front pews would sometimes be in the Nave, and sometimes moved away. No rules have been broken, but certainly in the time I have been involved (since 2002) I have not seen the front pews in their correct place – they are now left permanently in the South Transept.

+ The reason for moving these pews was to ‘make things cosy’ (the leaders would probably choose different words!) so that the choir would sit right by the congregation at the main 10am service, and the elements (bread and wine) would be consecrated at the nave altar. The modernisers seem to think that this idea will appeal to many. I contend that this is not the case – that the Choir should be in the Choir stalls, not parading in front of the congregation like a bunch of animals in a zoo (primarily they should be heard, and I don’t agree that they cannot be heard from the Chancel – they were heard between 1879 and 1999 and if they can’t be heard now then they either need to ‘sing up’ a bit or recruit more souls). Similarly the Minister is there to offer prayer and praise to God on behalf of the congregation, so he should be separated from them and at the High Altar. These days there are microphone systems to assist those with quiet voices, but ideally a Minister should declaim in received speech so that all may hear and understand.

If we retain the pews then this is the sort of church we can expect to get back to. A church with its own distinct traditional identity, not one in competition (and which does a very poor imitation of) the Baptist Church, the Ashford Christian Fellowship etc. These Churches do much good in the Community, and we can be united with them in the aim of supporting the whole Community, but the way in which the Orthodox Established Church (that’s us!) should be expressing this is very different from the non-conformist way. The one way in which we may not unite, and which I do not believe that God would want us to unite in, is worship. Look around the world; God loves diversity. We risk losing our identity and our heritage by allowing these pews to be removed, the knock-on effect of which will be permitting the building to be used in ways for which it was never intended.

What I am saying (particularly to those vitally important individuals in the town who do not worship regularly at the Parish Church, but for whom this Parish Church is still very dearly loved) is: “Please support our cause. Please help us to preserve this building to the uses for which it is intended. Please support the preservation of our beautiful pews so that future generations may marvel at the real and timeless beauty of the building (which I’m sure you all appreciate), so that our children, grandchildren and great-grandchildren may see with their own eyes and experience the awe, majesty and glory of God in reality as expressed by the traditional Established Church, and not just through a dusty photograph album”. I offer my personal conviction too that I personally could build a vibrant church community in the Parish Church (not that I am likely to be given the chance!) if I were given charge of the affairs of Ashford Parish Church. I say this only to illustrate how sure I am that the traditional way is the right way to build a strong network of friends of the Church – both those who worship, and those in the wider community. This is precisely what Canon N.M.G.. Sharp (Vicar 1955 –72) achieved. Such successes may still be found even now: the Parish Church of the small village of Nutfield in Surrey (where the Rector has just retired after 50 years in post) which boasts a strong choir and congregation who will continue their Rector’s good work, also at St Michael Cornhill in London, where the current Rector brought back traditionalism and his choir and congregation flourish.


Thank you all so very much for your continuing support at this time.

CHRISTOPHER J. COOPER

(Founder and co-ordinator)
