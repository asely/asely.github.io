Centenary of London No.1 Surprise Royal at St Dunstan, Stepney; 11th November 2007.

It must have been about six years ago that I originally realised that a very important centenary was to occur in the year 2007 for the bells of St Dunstan, Stepney, and in 2002 I booked the bells for the date of 11th November 2007. On that day, one hundred years before, ten members of the Ancient Society of College Youths met and rang the very first peal of London Surprise Royal. Little did they realise the controversy which this peal would stir, mainly because, as is now widely known, a true extension of London from Major to Royal is impossible. Somewhere along the way there has to be a ‘fudge’ in the method. The method & composition used in the 1907 peal which the College Youths rang was created by Gabriel Lindoff of Dublin, and involved putting the ‘fudge’ when the treble is in 3-4. Soon a great controversy was raging in the pages of ‘The Bell News & Ringers Record’, with other variations of London extended to Royal being propagated, especially by Rev. H. Law James and his brother Rev. E. Bankes James. Of course, both Lindoff and the band who rang the 1907 peal were incensed at such suggestion, and felt that as they had rung the method and named it as London S. Royal, and as no true extension of Royal from Major was possible, the variation they had rung was as valid as any other. Rev. H. Law James, on the other hand, contended that, because his extension of the method had the ‘fudge’ when the treble was in 9-0, this made his variation the closest to a true extension, and thus his method should be the one rung and named London Surprise Royal! After months of acrimonious letters to ‘The Bell News’ from all parties, the situation was brought to a Central Council Meeting and allowed “68 peal points”, but no other decision seems to have been taken at the time – the decision to name the variations as London No.1, No.2, No.3 etc was taken years later after the ringing of No.2 at Brighton and No.3 at Beddington.


Returning to the present time, it was fairly obvious that in order to mark the centenary of the first peal, and on quite such a tricky – yet very finely toned – ring of bells as Stepney, in order to allow the peal to be completed before 9pm. The Rector very generously agreed to alter the usual course of the services that day, so that Evensong would commence at 4:30pm, and a start could be made for the peal by 5:30pm.


Of course the Great War did not occur until 1914, and so no-one could know at that time that 11th November would become Armistice Day. This also had to be taken into account when arranging the 2007 peal. Compromise was realised in the arrangement of a half-muffled Quarter Peal of Grandsire Caters before the 10am Parish Mass. This was, unfortunately, unsuccessful due to a shift in course, but nonetheless, the ringing was well executed, and did much to set the tone of Armistice Day for the Parishioners.


Having thus honoured the dead through the half-muffled morning ringing, the muffles were removed and the bells rung open prior to Evensong, with the service ‘touch’ being some well-struck call-changes on ten including four of the peal band. The usual Evensong congregation was augmented by ringers who had come to listen to the peal attempt, and also by the six members of the peal band who had managed to fight their way through heavy traffic in time!


At the conclusion of Evensong, the Rector managed to spring a surprise; as it was my last official engagement at Stepney church he presented me with a large amount of bottled beer as a token of thanks for the eight years during which I have done my best to provide a band for occasions in the church calendar and weddings. Happily, a local band are now in training; regularly practising on Thursday evening, and ringing for Sunday services under their new Captain, Jonathan Slack.


The peal itself was especially enjoyable, given the historical link, and rather a good swan-song at a personal level. “Success is the reward of perseverance”. My personal opinion is that London No.1 is indeed what I would call ‘real London’, and I found it a most enjoyable method to ring. The general opinion of the band appeared to be that they enjoyed the method, and a modern composition would bring up a greater variety of music than that in the original composition by Lindoff, which we rang.


Here I must tender thanks to the very many people, without whom these arrangements would have come to naught. Firstly, best thanks to the Rector, both for the use of the bells and for altering the course of services to allow the peal attempt to proceed. Many thanks also to the new Captain, Jonathan Slack, and Vice Captain, Elizabeth Le Moine for all the effort they put in to the days proceedings, especially removing the muffles from the bells, and to Elizabeth for opening and closing the tower. To the band who rang in the Quarter Peal attempt in the morning, and especially Sarah Taylor and Leon Thompson for ringing in this and their general hospitality and helpfulness over the weekend. Also to the conductor of the morning Quarter Peal attempt, Mark Chittenden-Pile, who came to London especially for this, and then kindly conveyed me home, and to Hugo Tracey for recording the final hour of the peal. Grateful thanks must also be tendered to each and every member of the peal band for arriving promptly and ringing steadily, and not least to our esteemed conductor on this occasion, David C. Brown. Most of the band had travelled a considerable distance, and with such a late finish in prospect, this meant an added inconvenience of arriving home very late. I hope I have not omitted to thank anyone who contributed to making this such a worthy occasion.


It is hoped that a peal board may now be purchased and hung in the tower at Stepney, recording both the 1907 and 2007 peals, as no board now exists for the 1907 peal – I believe the original board hung on the wall in the College Youths Meeting Room at ‘The Coffee Pot’, Warwick Lane E.C., and was lost along with many other valuable items of Society property when that building was destroyed by enemy action.
